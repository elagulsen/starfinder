FAQs

How does it work?

The Star Finder takes into account your latitude and longitude, and matches it to Sky Quality Meter measurements taken around your area.
These measurements are used to determine how bright, or light polluted, the night sky is in your area.
Based on this information, as well as the position of the stars on the day you access this website, the Star Finder will generate a list of stars it thinks you might be able to see outside on a clear night!

This doesn't seem right. I can see more/less stars where I am!

The number of stars you can see is an estimation, and can vary depending on factors like sky brightness, weather, and more.
Star Finder does its best to estimate how many stars you would be able to see on a clear, cloudless night, on average in your area.

Why doesn't it work for my location?

Unfortunately, not every location has Sky Quality Meter data available! In particular, there is a lot of data for the US and Europe, but not a lot in other areas of the world.
If you want to engage in citizen science and contribute to light pollution research by taking measurements using just your phone, check out the Dark Sky Meter website!

Where did you get the data from?

The sky quality meter data is from Dark Sky Meter, which you can access here. Dark Sky Meter was developed by Norbert Schmidt and Harro Treur.
The star data was taken from the HYG database, which you can access here.
